If you run a gas turbine, most of the energy in your fuel leaves the exhaust stack as heat. An **HRSG boiler** — heat recovery steam generator — captures that heat and turns it into useful steam. Combined with a cogeneration turbine or a steam turbine, it is the backbone of efficient combined-cycle and CHP plants. This article explains what an HRSG is and why it matters.

An HRSG boiler is a waste heat recovery boiler that sits in the exhaust stream of a gas turbine. Instead of burning fuel, it uses the turbine's hot exhaust to boil water into steam across multiple pressure levels — high, intermediate, and low. No combustion means no extra fuel and very low emissions; you are simply recovering energy that would otherwise be thrown away.

The HRSG differs from a conventional fired boiler in two ways. First, it is fed by exhaust gas, not a burner, so it must handle large, variable gas flows. Second, modern HRSGs use supplemental firing (duct burners) to raise steam when demand peaks. They are typically water-tube designs with finned tubes to maximize heat pickup in a compact footprint.

In cogeneration, the steam from the HRSG boiler drives a steam turbine for additional power (combined cycle) or feeds process and heating loads (CHP). A **cogeneration turbine** downstream of the HRSG can lift total plant efficiency from roughly 40% (gas turbine alone) to 55–85% depending on how much heat you use. That is why HRSGs are standard in efficient power and industrial plants.

The business case is straightforward: an HRSG pays back through extra electricity and avoided fuel for separate steam generation. Key variables are exhaust temperature, steam pressure needed, and whether you can use the low-pressure steam on-site. Proper pinch-point design and regular tube inspection protect that return over the plant's life.

When specifying, match the HRSG to your gas turbine exhaust and your steam demand, then plan for inspection and spare tubes.
