The single most important decision when buying a steam turbine is what to do with the exhaust steam. Pick wrong and you either waste heat you needed or sacrifice electricity you could have sold. This article compares the two core options — the **condensing steam turbine** and the **back pressure steam turbine** — so you can match the machine to your plant.

A **condensing steam turbine** exhausts low-pressure steam into a surface condenser. Cooling water or air condenses the steam back to liquid, creating a deep vacuum that lets the steam expand as far as possible. The result is the highest possible electrical output per kilogram of steam. That makes condensing units the default for grid-connected and utility power plants, where electricity is the only product.

A **back pressure steam turbine** takes the opposite approach. It exhausts steam at a pressure high enough for process use — heating, drying, sterilization, or district heating. You give up some electricity, but you keep the heat, which is exactly what a factory with steam demand wants. Back-pressure units dominate CHP applications in sugar, paper, textile, and food processing.

If you need both, an **extraction-condensing turbine** bleeds steam at an intermediate pressure for process while condensing the remainder for power. It costs more and is more complex, but it is the most flexible answer when heat and power demands swing through the year.

| Factor | Condensing | Back-pressure |
|------|------|------|
| Electricity output | Highest | Lower (heat retained) |
| Best when | Selling power to grid | Plant also needs steam |
| Typical use | Utility plants | CHP factories |
| Overall fuel use | Lower electrical eff. | Up to 80%+ total eff. |

To choose: list your steam source, your power need, and whether your process consumes steam. If the answer is "power only," choose condensing. If "power + steam," choose back-pressure. If both vary, consider extraction-condensing.
