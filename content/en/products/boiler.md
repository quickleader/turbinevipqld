## How It Works

A boiler releases the heat locked in fuel and transfers it to water to raise steam. In a fired boiler, fuel burns in the furnace and the hot flue gas travels through convective heating surfaces — economizer, evaporator, superheater and reheater — where heat is absorbed step by step. In a heat recovery steam generator (HRSG) there is no burner; the boiler captures useful heat from gas-turbine exhaust instead (see our gas turbine solutions). The steam produced drives a turbine-generator for power, supplies process heat, or both in a CHP scheme. Combustion-air, feedwater-treatment and draft systems close the loop and determine how efficiently the unit converts fuel into usable steam, while the stack-gas train handles emission compliance.

## Efficiency & Technology

Boiler efficiency is driven by combustion control, excess-air management and how completely the flue gas is cooled before the stack. We specify economizers and air preheaters to recover low-grade heat, superheaters and reheaters to raise steam quality, and — for biomass — robust grate and fuel-feeding systems matched to fuel moisture and ash. For HRSG units, the pinch-point and approach-temperature design sets how much steam you recover from turbine exhaust; a well-designed HRSG can lift a combined-cycle plant to roughly 50–55% efficiency. We also integrate flue-gas desulfurization, denitrification and dust-removal to meet local emission limits, and we model fuel variability so the rated evaporation holds under real operating conditions.
