## How a Steam Turbine Works
A steam turbine converts the thermal energy of pressurized steam into rotational mechanical energy. High-pressure steam produced by a boiler expands through a series of fixed stator vanes and rotating blades, pushing the rotor. The rotor shaft drives a generator to produce electricity, or a compressor or pump for process duty. The way exhaust steam is handled defines the two main configurations: a **condensing steam turbine** exhausts low-pressure steam into a surface condenser (maximum electrical output), while a **back-pressure steam turbine** exhausts steam at a useful pressure for process heating, drying, or district heating — the natural choice for cogeneration (CHP) plants.

## Steam Turbine Efficiency — What Moves the Needle
For a steam turbine for power plant duty, efficiency depends on inlet steam temperature and pressure, condenser vacuum, blade aerodynamic design, and the clearance between rotating and stationary parts. Modern condensing units reach isentropic efficiencies above 85–90%, while well-maintained back-pressure machines in CHP service deliver overall plant fuel efficiency above 80% because the exhaust heat is not wasted. Our turbine retrofitting service recovers lost output by upgrading control systems, replacing worn blades, and re-machining seals — often restoring 3–8% of original output.
