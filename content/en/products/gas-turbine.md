## How It Works

A gas turbine converts the chemical energy of fuel into mechanical work. Ambient air is drawn into a multi-stage axial compressor and raised to high pressure, then mixed with fuel — natural gas, distillate or associated gas — and ignited in the combustion chamber. The resulting hot, high-pressure gas expands through the turbine stages to spin the shaft. That shaft drives a generator to produce electricity, while the exhaust gas, still at roughly 450–600 °C, carries large amounts of recoverable heat. In a simple-cycle configuration this exhaust is vented or used for low-grade heating; in a combined-cycle or CHP configuration it feeds a heat recovery steam generator (see our boiler solutions) to make steam for a steam turbine or process, raising overall efficiency dramatically.

## Efficiency & Technology

Simple-cycle gas turbines in this band typically return 30–42% electrical efficiency, depending on the class and ambient conditions. The biggest efficiency lever is what you do with the exhaust: adding an HRSG for combined-cycle duty lifts plant efficiency toward 50–55%, while CHP schemes that use both power and steam can exceed 80% fuel utilization. Other technology factors we manage include dry-low-NOx (DLN) combustors for emission compliance, cooled first-stage blades and vanes for higher firing temperatures, and intelligent governing and control that holds frequency and load during transient grid events. For sites with variable fuel quality we size fuel-treatment and filtration to protect the hot gas path, and we model part-load and ambient derating so the quoted output matches your real site, not a catalogue peak.
